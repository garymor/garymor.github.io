How it works?

The volumetric changes in concrete structures due to the loss of moisture by evaporation are known as concrete casting shrinkage.

When shrinkage comes up against a mechanical constraint, the concrete may crack – this will result with weakening of the casted element. Concrete shrinkage can be classified to a few types, among them “Plastic” and “Drying”.

Plastic shrinkage is considered as “immediate”. It occurs very soon after pouring the concrete into the forms. The hydration of cement results in a reduction in the volume of concrete due to evaporation from the surface of concrete, which leads to cracking. Drying shrinkage is considered as “long term”. It occurs after the setting and hardening of the concrete mixture due to the loss of capillary water. This process duration is measured in months and decreases with time.

How ARC - S works? Concrete cracks results from different types of shrinkage mechanisms that share the conflict between volume reduction and external forces that are applied due to hydration and water evaporation. By “Looking inside” during excess water evaporation - an “air/water” interface is formed with its associated capillaries, or in simple word known as “pore”. Since water possess a higher surface tension than air – a stress is formed on the internal walls of the pores and cause an inward pulling force that close this pore and leads to a crack. ARC - S interferes with the chemistry of the air/water or pore interface by reducing the surface tension and by thus lowering the volumetric shrinkage during water evaporation.
